Joschka Wiegleb

PhD Environmental Science

How to develop our environment in future is one of the challenging questions of our time. With my research, I provide basic evidence for sustainable development of aquatic ecosystems. I aim at understanding fish swimming behaviour in challenging flow fields. Combination of ecology, hydraulic engineering, and Artificial Intelligence allows for deep understanding of the underlying mechanisms of swimming in flowing water. During my PhD, I investigated the swimming behaviour of different fish species using video analysis, Artificial Intelligence, force measurements, and Acoustic Doppler velocimetry measurements to describe how fish species behave differently in fish passes. This investigation was basic for the description of a prototype hydraulic barrier, which aimed at impeding upstream migration of the invasive round goby, while allowing passage of native fish species.

I am a Postdoc.Mobility (SNSF) fellow at the International Centre for Ecohydraulic Research, University of Southampton, United Kingdom.

Personal information

Invasive species are a fundamental challenge for the modern management of aquatic ecosystems. While aquatic invasions are commonly caused by human activities, the impacts on ecosystems can be disastrous and hardly fixable. The modern society is challenged with the need of adequate management strategies. Is removal of those species from invaded habitats an adequate option? How can prevent further dispersal? What are short- and long-term effects of invasions on the ecosystems? Might there be advantages of aquatic invasions in some cases? When do invasive species have to be assumed to be an integrated part of the ecosystem? And at least: What is a natural ecosystem?

    PostDoc in Basel

    I worked as a PostDoc at the MGU, University of Basel, Switzerland, since from 01/2022 until 01/2023. My research was focused on management of invasive fish, analysis of swimming behaviour of fish and invertebrates using Artificial Intelligence. My teaching activities include the 'Tools and Methods' seminar, the 'Critical Reading' seminar, and the organization of the 'MSD Life Science Kolloquium' of the Master of Sustainable Development.

    PhD student

    I began with my dissertation in December 2017 and defended successfully on 6th January 2022. In my thesis, I bridged different disciplines and combined hydraulics, physics, and aquatic ecology to explore the factors that determine passage of different benthic fish species across river obstacles. Beside basic research evidence, I assessed the performance of a hydraulic, protective barrier to prevent upstream passage of the invasive round goby, while allowing passage of native species, such as gudgeon and bullhead. The prototype hydraulic barrier has been installed in a real fish pass already. The thesis was supervised by Prof. Dr. Patricia Holm, Prof. Dr. Peter Huggenberger, and Prof. Dr. Jost Borcherding as external expert.
